template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>in <a href="/wiki/physics" title="physics">physics</a>, <b>spacetime</b> is any <a href="/wiki/mathematical_model" title="mathematical model">mathematical model</a> which fuses the <a href="/wiki/three-dimensional_space" title="three-dimensional space">three dimensions of space</a> and the one <a href="/wiki/dimension" title="dimension">dimension</a> of <a href="/wiki/time" title="time">time</a> into a single <a href="/wiki/four-dimensional" class="mw-redirect" title="four-dimensional">four-dimensional</a> <a href="/wiki/manifold" title="manifold">manifold</a>. <a href="/wiki/minkowski_diagram" class="mw-redirect" title="minkowski diagram">spacetime diagrams</a> can be used to visualize <a href="/wiki/special_relativity" title="special relativity">relativistic</a> effects, such as why different observers perceive differently where and when events occur.\n</p><p>until the 20th century, it was assumed that the three-dimensional geometry of the universe (its spatial expression in terms of coordinates, distances, and directions) was independent of one-dimensional time. the famous physicist albert einstein helped develop the idea of space-time as part of his <a href="/wiki/theory_of_relativity" title="theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a>. prior to his pioneering work, scientists had two separate theories to explain physical phenomena: isaac newton\'s laws of physics described the motion of massive objects, while james clerk maxwell\'s electromagnetic models explained the properties of light. however, in 1905, <a href="/wiki/albert_einstein" title="albert einstein">albert einstein</a> based <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/translation:on_the_electrodynamics_of_moving_bodies" class="extiw" title="wikisource:translation:on the electrodynamics of moving bodies">a work on special relativity</a> on two postulates:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the laws of physics are <a href="/wiki/invariant_(physics)" title="invariant (physics)">invariant</a> (i.e., identical) in all <a href="/wiki/inertial_frame_of_reference" title="inertial frame of reference">inertial systems</a> (i.e., non-accelerating frames of reference)</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/speed_of_light" title="speed of light">speed of light</a> in a <a href="/wiki/vacuum" title="vacuum">vacuum</a> is the same for all observers, regardless of the motion of the light source.</li></ul>\n<p>the logical consequence of taking these postulates together is the inseparable joining together of the four dimensions\xe2\x80\x94hitherto assumed as independent\xe2\x80\x94of space and time. many counterintuitive consequences emerge: in addition to being independent of the motion of the light source, the speed of light is constant regardless of the frame of reference in which it is measured; the distances and even temporal ordering of pairs of events change when measured in different <a href="/wiki/inertial_frame_of_reference" title="inertial frame of reference">inertial frames of reference</a> (this is the <a href="/wiki/relativity_of_simultaneity" title="relativity of simultaneity">relativity of simultaneity</a>); and the linear additivity of velocities no longer holds true.\n</p><p>einstein framed his theory in terms of <a href="/wiki/kinematics" title="kinematics">kinematics</a> (the study of moving bodies). his theory was an advance over <a href="/wiki/history_of_special_relativity#lorentz&#39;s_1904_model" title="history of special relativity">lorentz\'s 1904 theory of electromagnetic phenomena</a> and <a href="/wiki/history_of_special_relativity#poincar\xc3\xa9&#39;s_dynamics_of_the_electron" title="history of special relativity">poincar\xc3\xa9\'s electrodynamic theory</a>. although these theories included equations identical to those that einstein introduced (i.e., the <a href="/wiki/lorentz_transformation" title="lorentz transformation">lorentz transformation</a>), they were essentially ad&#160;hoc models proposed to explain the results of various experiments\xe2\x80\x94including the famous <a href="/wiki/michelson%e2%80%93morley_experiment" title="michelson\xe2\x80\x93morley experiment">michelson\xe2\x80\x93morley interferometer experiment</a>\xe2\x80\x94that were extremely difficult to fit into existing paradigms.\n</p><p>in 1908, <a href="/wiki/hermann_minkowski" title="hermann minkowski">hermann minkowski</a>\xe2\x80\x94once one of the math professors of a young einstein in z\xc3\xbcrich\xe2\x80\x94presented a geometric interpretation of special relativity that fused time and the three spatial dimensions of space into a single four-dimensional continuum now known as <a href="/wiki/minkowski_space" title="minkowski space">minkowski space</a>. a key feature of this interpretation is the formal definition of the spacetime interval. although measurements of distance and time between <a href="/wiki/event_(relativity)" title="event (relativity)">events</a> differ for measurements made in different reference frames, the spacetime interval is independent of the inertial frame of reference in which they are recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>minkowski\'s geometric interpretation of relativity was to prove vital to einstein\'s development of his 1915 <a href="/wiki/general_relativity" title="general relativity">general theory of relativity</a>, wherein he showed how <a href="/wiki/stress%e2%80%93energy_tensor" title="stress\xe2\x80\x93energy tensor">mass and energy</a> <a href="/wiki/riemann_curvature_tensor" title="riemann curvature tensor">curve</a> flat spacetime into a <a href="/wiki/pseudo-riemannian_manifold" title="pseudo-riemannian manifold">pseudo-riemannian manifold</a>.\n</p><p><span class="anchor" id="contents">: 
In physics, spacetime is any mathematical model which fuses the three dimensions of space and the one dimension of time into a single four-dimensional manifold. Spacetime diagrams can be used to visualize relativistic effects, such as why different observers perceive differently where and when events occur.\n

A mathematical model is a description of a  system using mathematical concepts and language. The process of developing a mathematical model is termed mathematical modeling. Mathematical models are used in the natural sciences (such as physics, biology, earth science, chemistry) and engineering disciplines (such as computer science, electrical engineering), as well as in non-physical systems such as the social sciences (such as economics, psychology, sociology, political science). Mathematical models are also used in music,&#91;1&#93; linguistics,&#91;2&#93; \nphilosophy (for example, intensively in analytic philosophy), and religion (for example, the recurring uses of the #7, 12 &amp; 40 in the Bible).\n

locate a point within these spaces.\n</p><p>in <a href="/wiki/classical_mechanics" title="classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>, <a href="/wiki/space" title="space">space</a> and <a the world is a <a href="/wiki/four-dimensional_space" title="four-dimensional space">four-dimensional space</a> but not the one that was found necessary to describe <a href="/wiki/electromagnetism" title="electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>. the four dimensions (4d) of <a href="/wiki/spacetime" title="spacetime">spacetime</a> consist of <a href="/wiki/event_(relativity)" title="event (relativity)">events</a> that are not absolutely defined spatially and temporally, but rather are known relative to the motion of an <a href="/wiki/observer_(special_relativity)" title="observer (special relativity)">observer</a>. <a href="/wiki/minkowski_space" title="minkowski space">minkowski space</a> first approximates the universe without <a href="/wiki/gravity" title="gravity">gravity</a>; the <a href="/wiki/pseudo-riemannian_manifold" title="pseudo-riemannian manifold">pseudo-riemannian manifolds</a> of <a href="/wiki/general_relativity" title="general In physics and mathematics, the dimension of a mathematical space (or object) is informally defined as the minimum number of coordinates needed to specify any point within it.&#91;1&#93;&#91;2&#93; Thus a line has a dimension of one (1D) because only one coordinate is needed to specify a point on it&#160;&#8211;&#32;for example, the point at 5 on a number line. A surface such as a plane or the surface of a cylinder or sphere has a dimension of two (2D) because two coordinates are needed to specify a point on it&#160;&#8211;&#32;for example, both a latitude and longitude are required to locate a point on the surface of a sphere. The inside of a cube, a cylinder or a sphere is three-dimensional (3D) because three coordinates are needed to locate a point within these spaces.\n
